How to Analyze Financial Ratios

Financial ratio analysis is an essential step in understanding a company’s financial health. Whether you are an entrepreneur, student, or investor, knowing how to interpret these indicators helps you make better decisions.

A financial ratio only has meaning within its context. For example, a liquidity ratio of 2 may seem excellent, but in some industries where cash flows are fast, a ratio of 1.2 may be sufficient. That is why analysis should never be done in isolation.

Financial ratios are generally divided into four main categories:

  • Liquidity ratios: measure the ability to meet short-term obligations
  • Leverage ratios: assess the level of debt and risk
  • Efficiency ratios: analyze operational efficiency
  • Profitability ratios: measure the ability to generate profit

To properly analyze a company, it is important to compare ratios over time, compare them with similar companies, analyze multiple ratios together, and take the overall context into account.

1. Compare Ratios Over Time

Analyzing multiple periods helps identify trends such as declining profitability or gradual improvement in financial structure.

2. Compare with the Industry

Ratios vary significantly by industry. A manufacturing company and a service company will not have the same benchmarks.

3. Analyze Multiple Ratios Together

A company can be profitable but still lack liquidity. Ratios should always be analyzed as a whole.

4. Consider the Context

A growing company may temporarily show weaker ratios without this necessarily being negative.

Which Financial Ratios to Use Based on Your Business

Not all financial ratios are relevant in every situation. The choice depends on your type of business, your industry, and your objectives.

SMEs and Small Businesses

Small businesses should prioritize liquidity ratios.

  • Working Capital
  • Quick Ratio

Growing or Leveraged Companies

Leverage ratios become a priority.

  • Debt Ratio
  • Equity Ratio
  • Interest Coverage Ratio

Commercial Businesses

Efficiency ratios become very important for tracking cash flows.

  • Accounts Receivable Collection Period
  • Accounts Payable Payment Period

Performance-Driven Companies

Profitability ratios help evaluate overall efficiency.

  • Gross Profit Margin
  • Net Income
  • Return on Assets (ROA)
  • Return on Equity (ROE)

Practical Example of Financial Ratio Analysis

Let’s take a simple example to illustrate the analysis.

Company Data

  • Revenue: 500 000 $
  • Cost of Goods Sold: 300 000 $
  • Net Income: 60 000 $
  • Total Assets: 400 000 $
  • Total Liabilities: 200 000 $

Profitability Analysis

  • Gross Margin: 40 %
  • Net Margin: 12 %
  • ROA: 15 %
  • ROE: 40 %

Financial Analysis

  • Debt Ratio: 50 %
  • Equity Ratio: 1,33
  • Working Capital Ratio: 1,5

Conclusion

The company is profitable, stable, and relatively well managed, but it could still improve the speed at which it collects payments from customers.
